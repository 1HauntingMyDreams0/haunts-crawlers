General Isopod Care

All isopods require a balance of moisture, airflow, and organic material to thrive. A simple enclosure with proper substrate and leaf litter will support a healthy colony.

Enclosure

Use a ventilated plastic or glass container. Ensure airflow while maintaining humidity.

Substrate

A mix of organic soil, decayed wood, and leaf litter works best. This also acts as a food source.

Moisture

Keep one side of the enclosure moist and the other side drier. This allows isopods to regulate themselves.

Food

Leaf litter should always be available. Supplement with vegetables, fish flakes, or calcium sources like cuttlebone.

Temperature

Most species thrive at typical room temperatures (65–75°F).

Florida Orange

Difficulty: Beginner

Florida Orange is a hardy and active species, making it great for beginners.

Humidity

Prefers moderate humidity with a clear moisture gradient.

Behavior

Active and visible, often seen exploring the enclosure.

Notes

Very forgiving species that adapts well to most setups.

Glacier

Difficulty: Intermediate

Glacier isopods prefer slightly cooler and more stable conditions.

Humidity

Keep humidity consistent but avoid overly wet conditions.

Behavior

Less active on the surface but thrives in well-maintained setups.

Notes

More sensitive to changes, so stability is key.

Porcellio laevis "Peraccae"

Difficulty: Beginner

A fast-growing and hardy species known for its adaptability.

Humidity

Prefers a drier setup compared to many species, with access to a moist area.

Behavior

Very active and fast-moving, often visible.

Notes

Great for cleanup crews and breeding projects due to rapid reproduction.
